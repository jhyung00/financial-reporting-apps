The Core Challenge

In a world of infinite data, how do we find a company's true worth?

The Goal: To cut through market noise, biased reporting, and complex data to arrive at a defensible conclusion.

Every financial decision—from investment to corporate strategy—hinges on valuation. This requires more than just numbers; it requires a rigorous, systematic process.

STEP 1

Understanding the Business Environment

The Analyst's Strategic Toolkit

Strategic Toolkit

Before looking at a single number, we must understand the competitive landscape.

Assessing a company's performance without first considering its environment is inadvisable. We use three core frameworks:

  • Porter's Value Chain: How does the company create value?
  • Porter's Five Forces: What is the structure of industry competition?
  • SWOT Analysis: What are the internal and external realities?

Porter's Five Forces

The 5 Structural Drivers

  • Rivalry
  • Buyer Power
  • Supplier Power
  • Threat of Entry
  • Substitutes

Porter's 5 Forces: Real World Examples (Airlines)

Concept Check 1

Scenario: A new technology makes it easy for customers to switch from your product to a cheaper alternative without penalty. Which Force is primarily increasing?

A. Supplier Power
B. Threat of Substitution
C. Threat of Entry
Correct! When customers can switch to an alternative (like Zoom vs. Airlines for meetings), the Threat of Substitution rises, limiting pricing power.

Concept Check 2

Question: Which of the following increases the financial risk of a company?

A. Increasing the proportion of Debt (Liabilities)
B. Increasing the proportion of Equity
C. Increasing Cash holdings
Correct! Increasing debt increases mandatory interest and principal payments, raising the risk of bankruptcy if cash flows dip.

The Income Statement

Revenues - COGS - Expenses = Net Income

Revenues

Revenues

The total amount of money brought in from selling goods or services. Also called the "Top Line".

COGS

Cost of Goods Sold

Direct costs attributable to the production of the goods sold (e.g., Materials, Factory Labor).

Expenses

Operating Expenses

Overhead costs not directly tied to production, such as R&D, Marketing, and HQ Salaries.

Advanced: Noncontrolling Interest

When a company (Parent) owns >50% of a subsidiary, it consolidates 100% of the revenue, even if it doesn't own 100%.

Pfizer Example (2022)

Reported Net Income: $31,407M

Less: Income attributable to Noncontrolling Interest: ($35M)

Net Income to Pfizer Shareholders: $31,372M

Always use the value attributable to shareholders for valuation!

Decomposing ROA: Industry Context

You can achieve high returns in two ways. Click to see industry examples.

High Margin Strategy

Make a lot of profit on every unit sold, but sell fewer units.

(Reveal Industry)

Tech & Pharma

Example: Microsoft

Software has near-zero marginal cost.

Characteristics: High IP barriers, pricing power, lower turnover.

High Turnover Strategy

Make tiny profit per unit, but sell massive volume quickly.

(Reveal Industry)

Retail & Grocery

Example: Walmart

Groceries spoil; they must move fast.

Characteristics: Razor-thin margins, massive efficiency, high volume.

The Trade-Off

Companies usually sit on a curve. It is rare to have both High Margin and High Turnover.

Concept Check 3

Question: Company A has a Profit Margin of 2% and Asset Turnover of 4.0. Company B has a Profit Margin of 10% and Asset Turnover of 0.8. Which has the better ROA?

A. Company A
B. Company B
C. They are the same
Correct! ROA = Margin x Turnover.
Company A: 2% x 4.0 = 8%.
Company B: 10% x 0.8 = 8%.
