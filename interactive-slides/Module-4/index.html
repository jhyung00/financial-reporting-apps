The Market for Credit

Supply & Demand

Like any market, credit is governed by laws of supply and demand.

Class Discussion:

Who are the primary players in this market?

  • Demand: Companies (Operating, Investing, Financing activities).
  • Supply: Trade Creditors, Banks (Private), Public Debt Investors, Lease Financing.

Demand: Operating Activities

Companies have cyclical cash needs.

Key Drivers

  • Cyclical Needs: Manufacturers need cash for materials/labor before they sell the product.
  • Seasonality: Retailers buy inventory in Q3 to sell in Q4.
  • Operating Losses: Covering temporary negative cash flows.

Discussion Question:

Is lending to cover operating losses always "High Risk"?

Not necessarily. A willing lender could be the difference between bankruptcy and survival during a temporary downturn (e.g., COVID-19).

PP&E (CAPEX)

M&A

Demand: Financing Activities

Why borrow money?

  • Refinancing: Rolling over maturing debt.
  • Shareholder Returns: Stock Buybacks & Dividends.

Discussion: Why would a company borrow money just to buy back its own stock?

Leveraging EPS: Debt is often cheaper than Equity (Cost of Capital). Reducing share count boosts Earnings Per Share (EPS), often triggering executive bonuses.

Supply: Trade Credit

Trade (Supplier) Credit: Routine and usually non-interest bearing.

Suppliers specify:

  • Credit Limit (Max amount).
  • Payment Terms (e.g., Net 30).
  • Early Payment Discounts (e.g., 2/10 Net 30).

Critical Thinking:

Why is trade credit considered the "first line of defense"?

It's the easiest credit to get. If suppliers stop shipping (cut off credit), operations halt immediately. It signals deep distress.

Bank Loans: Revolving Credit

Definition

A "Revolver" allows a company to draw down cash when needed and repay it when cash comes in.

Analogy: What is the consumer equivalent of a Revolver?

A Credit Card. You have a limit, you borrow against it, you pay it back, and the credit becomes available again.

Bank Loans: Lines of Credit

Back-Up Facilities

Often "Standby" letters of credit. The bank guarantees funds will be available if needed.

  • Guaranty: Back-stops other obligations.
  • Liquidity Support: Backs up Commercial Paper.

Discussion: If a company suddenly draws down its entire Line of Credit, is that good news?

Usually NO.

It often means they are shut out of other markets (like Commercial Paper) and are hoarding cash to survive.

Bank Loans: Term Loans

Structure

A loan for a specific amount with a specified repayment schedule.

  • Use Case: Typically used to fund PP&E (Property, Plant, Equipment).
  • Duration: Matches the useful life of the asset.
  • Collateral: The asset usually serves as collateral.
Interest Rate: Floating (SOFR + Spread)
Amortization: Principal paid over time

SOFR: Secured Overnight Financing Rate. The "risk-free" benchmark rate banks use (replaced LIBOR).

Spread: The risk premium (e.g., +2%) added by the bank based on the borrower's credit quality.

Amortization: Paying off the loan principal gradually over the life of the loan (vs. a "Bullet" payment at the end).

Bank Loans: Mortgages

Specific financing for Real Estate transactions.

  • Security: The lender takes a lien on the specific property.
  • Terms: Generally longer-term (10-30 years).

Bankruptcy Context:

If the company goes bust, who gets the building?

The Mortgage Holder is a Secured Creditor. They get proceeds from the building sale before anyone else.

Other Financing: Leases

Lease Financing

Instead of buying, companies lease assets.

  • Finance Lease: Like a loan. You essentially buy the asset over time. (Asset + Liability on Balance Sheet).
  • Operating Lease: Like renting.

Airline Example:

Operating Lease: Delta rents a plane for 3 years. It returns it. (Historically off-balance sheet).

Finance Lease: Delta leases a plane for 20 years (its full life). It's effectively a loan to buy the plane.

Do Agencies Care?

Do rating agencies care if it's "Operating" vs "Finance"?

No. S&P treats almost ALL lease liabilities as Debt when calculating solvency ratios, regardless of accounting classification.

The Credit Risk Analysis Process

Purpose: To quantify expected credit losses.

Expected Loss = Chance of Default × Loss Given Default

Discussion

Why do creditors care more about downside risk than upside potential?

Because returns are Fixed (Interest payments). If the company doubles in value, the bank doesn't get paid double. But if it defaults, the bank loses everything.

Data Reliability

Before calculating ratios, we must standardize data. A classic example is the "53rd Week" anomaly.

Scenario: Retailers often end their fiscal year on the last Saturday of January. Every 5-6 years, this results in a 53-week year.

Comparing a 53-week year to a 52-week year without adjustment distorts growth rates (makes them look better than they are).

In-Class Exercise: 53rd Week Adjustment

Company: Kraft Heinz
Fiscal Year: 2022 (53 Weeks)
Reported Net Sales: $25,985 M

Calculation: Normalize 2022 to 52 weeks.

25,985 ×
52 53
= Adjusted Sales
Adjusted Net Sales: $25,491 M
Impact: Reveals a 2.1% decline vs. Reported flat sales

Pillar 1: Solvency

Question: How reliant is the company on debt versus equity financing?

Main ratios:

  1. Liabilities-to-Equity Ratio: All creditor financing.
  2. Total Debt-to-Equity Ratio: Interest-bearing debt only.

Industry Context:

Does high debt always mean bad management?

No. Telecom companies (like Verizon) typically run higher leverage than tech companies due to heavy infrastructure needs (towers, fiber).

In-Class Exercise: Solvency

Total Liabilities: $287,217 M
Stockholders' Equity: $92,463 M

Calculate Liabilities-to-Equity.

287,217 92,463
= 3.11

Interpretation: High leverage. For context, Comcast is ~2.1. Verizon uses $3.11 of debt for every $1 of equity.

Pillar 2: Coverage (TIE)

EBIT: $30,467 M
Interest: $3,613 M

Calculate Times Interest Earned (TIE).

30,467 3,613
= 8.43x

Interpretation

(Click to Flip)

8.43x is Strong.

It means Verizon can pay its interest expense 8 times over with its operating income.

Pillar 2: Coverage (EBITDA)

What if we add back Depreciation & Amortization ($17,099 M)?

30,467 + 17,099 3,613
= 13.17x

EBITDA coverage is always higher because it ignores non-cash expenses.

Pillar 2: Coverage (Cash Flow)

CFO: $37,141 M
CAPEX: $23,087 M
Total Debt: $176,331 M

Calculate Free Operating Cash Flow to Total Debt.

37,141 - 23,087 176,331
= 8%

Insight

While Earnings coverage (8.43x) looked huge, FCF coverage is tight (8%). This highlights the heavy capital cost of maintaining the network.

Pillar 3: Liquidity

Two Key Ratios

  • Current Ratio:
    Current Assets Current Liabilities
  • Quick Ratio:
    Cash + Marketable Sec + A/R Current Liabilities

Discussion 1: Why exclude Inventory?

It is the least liquid current asset.

Discussion 2: Can you come up with a real-life example of illiquid inventory?

Real Life Example: A fashion retailer having 10,000 winter coats in July cannot sell them quickly without a massive (70%+) discount to generate cash.

In-Class Exercise: Liquidity

Current Assets: $37,857 M
Current Liab: $50,171 M

Calculate Current Ratio.

37,857 50,171
= 0.75

Observation: < 1.0 means negative working capital. Common for efficient giants like Verizon, but risky for smaller firms.

What is Healthy?

  • Manufacturing: > 1.5 is standard.
  • Retail/Telecom: < 1.0 can be acceptable if cash flow is fast and consistent.

Cost of Debt

2. Access to Capital

Access to Capital

Many institutional investors (Pension funds) are restricted from buying "Non-Investment Grade" (Junk) debt (Below BBB- / Baa3).

The S&P Process

The Framework

  1. Business Risk Profile: Country risk, Industry risk, Competitive position.
  2. Financial Risk Profile: Cash flow & Leverage analysis.
  3. Anchor Rating: Combination of 1 & 2.
  4. Modifiers: Diversification, Liquidity, Management.

Adjustments: S&P adjusts reported numbers. Example: Verizon's reported debt of $152B was adjusted to $174B by S&P (adding leases/pensions).

Moody's Approach: Financial Metrics

Moody's defines risk classes based on median ratio values (2022).

Rating Debt / EBITDA EBITA / Interest FFO / Debt
Aaa 1.1x 21.0x 83.5%
Aa 1.3x 12.0x 65.4%
A 2.1x 15.1x 41.7%
Baa 2.6x 10.2x 32.6%
Ba (Junk) 3.3x 10.8x 24.9%
B 6.0x 7.2x 12.3%

Analyst Exercise:

Hypothetical Company X has: Debt/EBITDA = 1.3x and EBITA/Interest = 12.0x. What rating fits best?

Looking at the table, these metrics align almost perfectly with the Aa category (Debt/EBITDA 1.3x, EBITA/Interest 12.0x).

Comparison: S&P vs Moody's

  • Methodology: Both use proprietary models mixing Quant/Qualitative data.
  • Adjustments: Both make adjustments to GAAP/IFRS data (e.g., Leases as debt).
  • Terminology: S&P uses all caps (AAA), Moody's uses title case (Aaa).

Key Takeaway

While methodologies differ slightly, the outcome is usually correlated. A company rated A by S&P is rarely rated B by Moody's.

Predicting Bankruptcy: Altman Z-Score

A statistical model developed to predict bankruptcy probability within 2 years.

Z = 1.2A + 1.4B + 3.3C + 0.6D + 0.99E

Z > 3.00: Safe | 1.8 - 2.99: Gray | Z < 1.80: Distress

Exercise: Verizon Z-Score

Inputs Calculated: A=-0.04, B=0.18, C=0.08, D=0.65, E=0.35

-0.048 + 0.252 + 0.264 + 0.39 + 0.346 = 1.20

Verizon 1.2
Distress Gray Safe

Analyst's Dilemma

Verizon scores 1.2 (Distress) but is rated BBB+ (Inv. Grade). Why? Qualitative factors: Monopoly power, steady utility-like cash flow, access to capital.

Loss Given Default (LGD): Priority

LGD measures potential loss if default occurs. The critical factor is Priority of Claim.

1. Equity 2. Senior Secured Debt 3. Subordinated Debt 4. Senior Unsecured Debt

Payment Hierarchy

  • 1. Senior Secured Debt (Paid First)
  • 2. Senior Unsecured Debt
  • 3. Subordinated Debt
  • 4. Equity (Common/Preferred) - Paid Last

LGD: Limiting Exposure

Credit Limits

Capping the total exposure to a single borrower.

  • Banks: Set limits on revolvers and reduce them if ratings drop.
  • Trade Creditors: Start new customers with low limits.

Collateral

Securing the loan with specific assets (Real Estate, Equipment).

Warning: Collateral limits loss but doesn't eliminate it. Liquidating assets takes time and costs money.

LGD: Repayment Terms

The Risk/Reward Trade-off of Term Length.

Longer Term = Higher Risk

Chance of Default

Cost of Debt

Crucial Question: Does the economic life of the asset match the loan term?

LGD: Covenants

Loan terms designed to limit the lender's loss.

  • Affirmative: Things the borrower MUST do (e.g., Submit financial statements).
  • Restrictive: Things the borrower CANNOT do (e.g., No major mergers without approval).
  • Financial: Metrics they must maintain.

Real-World Covenant Examples:

"Leverage Ratio: The Borrower shall not permit the Consolidated Total Leverage Ratio as of the end of any Fiscal Quarter to be greater than 3.50 to 1.00."
"Negative Pledge: The Borrower will not create, incur, assume or suffer to exist any Lien upon any of its property, assets or revenues, whether now owned or hereafter acquired."

The Complete Picture: A Disciplined Process

Expected Credit Loss = Chance of Default × Loss Given Default

Chance of Default

Managed through a rigorous assessment of:

  • Borrower's Purpose
  • Environment
  • Financials
  • Future Prospects

Loss Given Default

Managed by proactively structuring the deal with:

  • Limits
  • Collateral
  • Terms
  • Covenants
Key Takeaway: Effective credit analysis systematically identifies and mitigates downside risk.

Beyond the Framework

The Art of Analyst Judgment

Numbers and covenants provide the map, but judgment provides the compass.

Success depends on interpreting the data, understanding business nuances, and applying experience.

  • Forecasts: Future cash flows repay debt.
  • Z-Score: Bankruptcy prediction (< 1.8 Distress).
  • Ratings: Mix of Quant & Qual factors.

Loss Given Default

  • Priority: Secured > Unsecured > Equity.
  • Structure: Covenants & Collateral limit loss.
