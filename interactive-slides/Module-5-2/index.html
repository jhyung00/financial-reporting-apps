The Hook: "The Phantom Profit"

Imagine a company that sells $1 Million in products. Profits look great!

But... the products were sold to customers who can't pay.

In Part 2, we learn how to spot these issues. We move from "Revenue" (Top Line) down to "Net Income", checking the quality of every deduction along the way.

Analyst Adjustments 5.2: Allowances

Managers have latitude. Analysts seek to "undo" deliberate variation.

The Method

  1. Calculate the Average Rate of (Allowance / Gross AR) over ~3 years.
  2. Apply that average rate to the current Gross AR to find the "Adjusted Allowance".
  3. Calculate the difference to see if Income was Over/Under stated.

Pfizer Adjustment Calculation

Pfizer's Average Allowance % (2020-2022) = 4.558%

2022 Gross A/R: $11,401
Reported Allowance (3.94%): $449.0
Adjusted Allowance (4.56%): $519.6
Difference (Shortfall): ($70.6)

Implication

Is 2022 Net Income Overstated or Understated?

Overstated.

A smaller allowance means less Bad Debt Expense was recorded, inflating income by ~$71M.

Acquired R&D (IPR&D)

When a company acquires another firm with promising R&D projects.

Accounting: Capitalize as an Asset (Intangible).

Reasoning: Fair value is established by the acquisition price.

Treatment

IPR&D is an Indefinite-Lived Intangible Asset.

It is NOT amortized until the project is completed or abandoned.

Why Segregate Discontinued Ops?

Transitory Items

These items will NOT recur. They are irrelevant for predicting future performance.

Investors focus on Income from Continuing Operations as the "Persistence" metric.

Criteria for Classification

1. Strategic Shift:
e.g., Pfizer spinning off its Upjohn generic drug business.

2. Major Effect:
e.g., The business unit accounted for ~20% of total revenue.

Analyst Adjustment 5.3

When calculating ROE or ROA for forecasting...

Do NOT Use

Net Income

If Discontinued Ops are present, Net Income includes "noise" from the divested business.

DO Use

Income from Continuing Operations

This captures the underlying, persistent operations of the firm.

Pro Forma (Non-GAAP) Reporting

Management often believes GAAP doesn't tell the whole story.

Real Life Example: Uber (2019)

Uber reported a GAAP Net Loss of $8.5 Billion.

But reported "Adjusted EBITDA" as a Loss of $2.7 Billion.

They added back Stock Based Compensation, Legal Settlements, and Depreciation to make the loss look smaller.

Discussion

What do you think are the potential problems of Non-GAAP reporting?

1. Lack of Standardization: Hard to compare across companies.

2. Opportunism: Management can exclude "bad" real costs to make results look better.

3. Confusion: Can mislead investors about core profitability.

SEC Warning on Pro Forma

"We wish to caution public companies on their use of this 'pro forma' financial information..."

  • Non-GAAP has no defined meaning.
  • Hard to compare across companies.
  • Can mislead if it obscures GAAP results.

"Read before you invest; understand before you commit."

Analyst Checklist

For a thorough reading of financials:

  1. Read the external audit report (look for deviations).
  2. Peruse notes on accounting policies (compare to peers).
  3. Examine changes in policies.
  4. Identify nonrecurring items.
  5. Recast financial statements (Adjust Allowances, Remove Disc Ops).

Summary: Quality of Earnings

Accounts Receivable

  • Net AR = Gross - Allowance.
  • Watch Aging and DSO trends.

Allowances

  • Susceptible to "Cookie Jar" accounting.
  • Compare Allowance % over time.

Operating Expenses

  • R&D: Expensed (Internal) vs Capitalized (Acquired).
  • Benchmark against peers.

Non-GAAP

  • Adjusted Earnings usually > GAAP.
  • Check the Reconciliation carefully.
